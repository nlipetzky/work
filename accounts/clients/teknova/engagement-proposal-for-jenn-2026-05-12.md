## How the work moves

Your infrastructure is in place. What is ahead is operating it ... each play runs on the same engine, and each one makes the engine sharper.

Every play has two parts:

- **The build.** Different domains need different parts of the engine added or extended ... new sources connected, new matching logic written, new vocabulary captured. The build varies with how unfamiliar the domain is. The build does not ship lists. It produces the conditions under which lists can be trusted.
- **The run.** Once the build is done, the play runs ... sourcing, qualifying, refining, sending. Each run improves the rules. Runs ship lists; the build does not.

One play is active at a time. A second starts once the first is past the build and running steadily.

## What the retainer covers

Your retainer gets you focused expertise on Teknova's outbound system. The focus goes to three things:

- **Moving the active play forward.** Each week, the play advances ... whether it is in the build or in the run.
- **One integration or system build per month outside the active play.** A new Salesforce sync pattern, an automation to summarize a Teknova account, a new reporting view. Requested as needed, scoped together. Beyond one per month, additional work would be a scope conversation.
- **A weekly update** covering what was done, where things stand, and what is coming next.

The play list itself is maintained from your priorities ... what you have asked for in our prior conversations stays on the list until we activate it or take it off together.

## The boundaries that make this work

The retainer covers a defined slice of my time and focus. Holding the cadence ... written updates, focused work between them, meetings only when there is a defined purpose ... is what keeps the slice on production. When the cadence breaks down with ad-hoc meetings or mid-cycle direction shifts, what compresses is the work itself, not the relationship around the work. The boundaries are part of how the retainer produces more value, not less.

A note on meetings: they work when they have a defined purpose ... a decision to make, an artifact to review, a problem to work through together. They do not work as recurring status check-ins, because the weekly update already covers status. When something specific needs face time, we name it and convene. When it does not, we do not. The calendar does not drive the meetings; the work does.
