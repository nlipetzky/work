## Why v0.1 → v0.2 changed

**v0.1 change:** Nick flagged that the v0 copy risked turning into a chat session rather than a booked call. Research across multiple data sets (Gong, Josh Braun, Lavender, 304K-email studies) is consistent: at the cold stage, interest-based CTAs convert 2-3x better than meeting/calendar asks. Asking for time triggers loss aversion in busy executives ... they default-protect their calendar.

The v0.1 structural fix:

1. **Killed the calendar link from the cold sequence entirely.** It moves to the manual reply after a yes.
2. **Led the connect note with an observation about THEIR world**, not Will's credentials. Will's profile carries the credentials; the note's job is to make the connection feel relevant.
3. **CTAs at every touch became interest gauges, not meeting asks.**
4. **When a prospect replies positively, Nick drops a manual reply** with two specific times and the Motion link. Two-touch progression (cold → interest → time confirm + link) is the research-backed flow.

**v0.2 change:** Convergent edits from the expert panel debrief (`clay-heyreach-copy-expert-panel-debrief-2026-05-27.md`). Panel: Josh Braun, Lavender (Will Allred), Gong (Devin Reed), Becc Holland, Belal Batrawy, Jason Bay.

- Touch 1: "Medical device robotics" pulled to first word (Lavender / Holland ... CEO needs to know in 5 chars this is about their world). CTA softened to "Worth comparing notes?" (Braun / Batrawy / Bay).
- Touch 2: Killed "Quick context" filler (Braun / Batrawy). Replaced "small group of operators who's been through FDA clearance for an AI medical device" with the leaner "I got FDA clearance for an AI medical device a few years back" (Braun). Swapped "commercial-trajectory notes" → "go-to-market notes" (Holland ... CEO vocabulary match). Simplified the pattern line (Lavender ... phone-read test).
- Touch 3: Dropped "Probably 20 minutes of value either direction" (Braun / Batrawy ... sneaks the time ask back in). Specified "worth comparing notes?" instead of "worth a yes?" (Lavender ... yes to what?).
- Touch 4: Narrowed "sales cycle, payer conversations, or commercial motion" → "go-to-market for a post-FDA-AI-clearance device" (Braun ... pick one).

This sells the conversation, not the meeting. The meeting follows from the conversation.

## What happens when a prospect says yes (manual step)

When a prospect replies "yes" / "interested" / "tell me more" / any positive signal, the sequence pauses (HeyReach auto-pause) and Nick (or Will) sends a manual reply.

**SLA: within 4 hours of the positive signal landing.** Per Jason Bay: the half-life on a "yes" reply is short. A reply that lands 24 hours later converts at a fraction of the rate of one that lands the same business day. If Nick can't hit 4 hours, the booking rate drops materially. Build the notification path for positive replies accordingly (HeyReach reply alerts → Nick's phone or Slack).

Template for the manual reply:

```
Appreciate it. Two options that work on my side:

- {{DAY_1}} at {{TIME_1_LOCAL}}
- {{DAY_2}} at {{TIME_2_LOCAL}}

If neither works, calendar's here: {{WILL_CALENDAR_URL}}. 20 minutes is plenty.
```

Two specific times reduces decision fatigue. Calendar link is the fallback, not the lead. 20 minutes (not 30) signals respect for their time.

If a prospect replies but it's not a clear yes (e.g., "what is this about?" or "send me more info"), Nick responds with a 2-3 sentence elaboration on the pattern, then re-offers the interest gauge. Don't pivot to selling.

## Research backing the v0.1 structure

Key findings from cold-outreach research (2025-2026):

- Interest-based CTAs converted 30% to booking vs 15% for specific CTAs at cold stage (304K-email study).
- Soft CTAs achieved 4.2% reply rate vs 1.4% for hard CTAs (book meeting / demo) ... 3x gap.
- Asking for time upfront triggered 44% lower reply rates due to loss aversion.
- Timeline hooks for CEO/founder audiences hit ~10% reply rate vs ~4% for problem hooks.
- Recommended progression: cold = interest CTA only, reply = propose 2 times, time confirmed = send calendar link.
