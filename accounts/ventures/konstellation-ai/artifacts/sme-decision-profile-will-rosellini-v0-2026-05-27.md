## Purpose

How Will thinks and decides. Slow vs. fast. Data-first vs. instinct-first. Frame-first vs. example-first. Risk posture. How he wants options presented. Drives how Hermes routes asks, how the operator presents trade-offs, and how Slot Reports are shaped.

## Content

### Speed: fast to act once clarity is there; willing to sit when the framework is still forming

Will has an explicit bias toward action: "I have a bias towards actions and I've been the least active in this partnership of any other partnership. I'm usually bugging the s*** out of people. I'm stuck a lot." When stuck, he wants Nick to feed him the missing structural piece.

But on framework-level decisions (catalog architecture, deterministic-rule scaffolding), he is patient: "I'm in no hurry and patiently going through this process."

Pattern: **fast on commercial execution, patient on systems design.**

### Basis: instinct-first, but reaches for validation in regulated markets

Will trusts his instincts on directional bets ... "I'm predicting [law margin collapse] goes way faster than people think." He's predicted multiple inflection points correctly (Patentvest disintermediation, the law firm collapse).

But in regulated markets (medical, legal, defense) he demands validation layers and rubrics: "What is the test for the requirement?" "There has to be a validation layer." "Canon is critical but no one even knows what that means."

Pattern: **gut for direction, data for proof.**

### Approach: frame-first

Will is decisively frame-first. He cannot operate inside abstract conversations: "What I was having trouble with is okay, what do I start talking about to generate the deterministic rules ... I got lost."

When Nick gave him the EOS-derived AI-first framework, he latched on immediately: "Because I have experience with [EOS], it fit immediately. The constellation [definitions] were new definitions and not that they were bad."

What this means in practice: present Will with a frame, then ask him to populate it. Don't ask him to invent the frame from a blank page.

### Risk posture: calculated venture risk; aggressive avoidance of "product risk" and declining industries

Will seeks asymmetric upside:
- SBIR data-rights arbitrage → Phase 3 sole-source contracts ($30M)
- Hardware-anchored AI → defensible IP moat
- "Flywheel to lose money at scale" (tax-advantaged R&D structures)

But refuses concentrated single-product risk: "Raphael's not the right guy. He wants to build it for his product, but I wouldn't want to take any product risk."

Refuses declining categories: "I'm not going to go buy anything. I don't want to get in an industry that's going away."

### Options presentation: definitive, not menu-style; he wants Nick to commit to the best practice

"I like to win. So, just tell me the best practice and I'll do whatever the best practice is. Don't customize it."

He does not want optionality fatigue. He wants Nick to pick the path, present it, and ask for go/no-go.

### Reversibility preference

Will commits readily to **reversible** experiments ("force the template ... if it works, great. If not, we pivot"). He hedges on irreversible commitments (acquisition without a CEO in place, hiring without revenue, taking on product risk).

He will pivot fast on outbound campaigns, pricing models, and segment focus. He will not pivot fast on equity terms, partnership shapes, or capital deployment.

### Information format preference

**Email is his primary surface.** "I'm hyperproductive. So, I'm zero inbox." He explicitly asked Nick to wire KAI's agentic systems to talk to him via email rather than chat UIs: "is there an email I can just email and say start doing this." Operating in writing is his native mode.

He prefers **concise, structured, action-tagged** writing. He likes Google Keep for capture; he'll accept whatever Nick says is the best practice.

### Decision-pace by category

- **Commercial calls / sales:** fast. "If calls show up on my calendar, I'll show up and sell it."
- **Pricing:** deliberate; routes through him; willing to test multiple shapes
- **Catalog / brand positioning:** patient; deferred to Nick on language he doesn't connect to
- **Engagement-level decisions (which client to take):** fast based on his instinct about market segment
- **Internal systems / tooling:** delegates entirely to Nick; "Boss me around"

### Honesty about what he doesn't know

Will plainly states his limits:
- "I don't know how to fill up my own calendar"
- "I don't know how to do a marketing campaign"
- "I have no technical expertise"
- "I don't know what is the venture-backed approach in terms of a moat. I don't know."
- "It doesn't matter what I think. I'm definitely wrong [about specific market timing]."

This is a strength for the partnership ... Will hands off the things he doesn't know to Nick, and Nick can route around them.

## Why this matters

Most Hermes-style failures come from asking a frame-first decision-maker for an example-first response, or asking a slow-pace decision-maker to commit fast. The Decision-Making Profile is the structural way to prevent that mismatch.

For Will specifically: do NOT send him open-ended "what do you think about X?" prompts. Always send him a frame, a recommendation, and a go/no-go ask.
