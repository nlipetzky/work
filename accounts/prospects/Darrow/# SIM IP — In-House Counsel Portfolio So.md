# SIM IP — In-House Counsel Portfolio Sourcing Workflow

**Target: Medical Device & Robotics Companies**
**Acquisition Thesis: $1B+ in infringed revenue from existing products**
**For: Marketing Associate**
**Owner: Will Rosellini**
**Updated: May 2026**

-----

## PURPOSE

Patent attorneys refer portfolios they’ve built. In-house counsel sell portfolios they no longer need.

Medical device and robotics companies are ideal SIM IP acquisition targets because they generate large patent portfolios through R&D cycles, but only commercialize a fraction of the patented technology. The rest sits dormant — often covering technologies that competitors, adjacent-market players, or entirely different industries are using without a license.

This workflow targets the VP/Head of IP, General Counsel, and CTO at medical device and robotics companies to identify portfolios where:

- The company owns 50+ patents
- The patented technology is practiced by third parties generating $1B+ in aggregate revenue
- The company has no active licensing program
- The patents have 5+ years of remaining life

This is a different motion than the attorney referral workflow. Attorneys are intermediaries. In-house counsel are decision-makers who control the asset. The sales cycle is longer, the deal size is larger, and the relationship requires a different register — you’re talking to a corporate executive about unlocking balance-sheet value, not a law firm partner about referral fees.

## PART 1: TARGET UNIVERSE

### Why Medical Devices & Robotics

**Medical devices:** High R&D spend, long patent life, heavy regulatory barriers that slow commercialization but don’t slow patent filing. Companies file broadly around platforms, then commercialize narrowly. The gap between what’s patented and what’s sold is enormous. Medtronic, Stryker, Boston Scientific, Intuitive Surgical, and Zimmer Biomet each hold 10,000+ patents. Mid-market companies ($100M-$2B revenue) hold 200-2,000 patents and almost never license.

**Robotics:** Rapidly expanding claim space with collision between surgical robotics, industrial automation, warehouse logistics, autonomous vehicles, and consumer robotics. A surgical robot patent might cover sensor fusion technology that Amazon’s warehouse robots infringe. The cross-industry licensing opportunity is where the $1B revenue threshold gets hit.

**The $1B test:** SIM IP’s acquisition thesis requires that the infringed revenue from existing products across all potential licensees exceeds $1B. In medtech and robotics, this is achievable when:

- The patent covers a platform technology (not a single product feature)
- The claims read on products in adjacent markets (not just direct competitors)
- Multiple companies across multiple sectors practice the technology
- The market is growing (IoT, AI, surgical automation, connected devices)

### Target Company Segmentation

#### Segment 1: Mid-Market Medtech ($100M-$2B revenue)

These companies have meaningful portfolios but lack the resources for in-house licensing programs. They file patents defensively and never monetize offensively.

**Target profile:**

- 200-2,000 issued U.S. patents
- R&D spend > 10% of revenue
- No Chief Licensing Officer or VP of Licensing on the org chart
- Products in: surgical instruments, implantables, imaging, diagnostics, patient monitoring, drug delivery devices

**Research signals that a portfolio qualifies:**

- Patent families with broad platform claims (not narrow product-specific claims)
- High forward citation counts (other companies are building on this technology)
- Claims that read on technologies outside medtech (sensor fusion, wireless communication, signal processing, data analytics)
- Company has pivoted strategy or exited a product line, leaving patents orphaned

#### Segment 2: Surgical & Medical Robotics

The surgical robotics market is projected to exceed $20B by 2028. Intuitive Surgical dominates but there are dozens of companies with foundational patents in control systems, haptic feedback, computer vision, and teleoperation.

**Target profile:**

- Companies that developed robotic platforms but didn’t achieve market dominance
- University spinouts with strong patent portfolios but limited commercialization
- Companies that were acquired (the acquirer often doesn’t license the legacy portfolio)
- Robotics companies that pivoted from medical to industrial or vice versa

#### Segment 3: Industrial & Logistics Robotics

These companies patent navigation, SLAM, manipulation, human-robot interaction, and sensor fusion — technologies practiced by every major logistics, manufacturing, and autonomous vehicle company.

**Target profile:**

- Companies with 100+ patents in autonomy, navigation, manipulation, or sensor fusion
- Failed or acquired robotics startups with strong IP (Rethink Robotics model)
- Defense contractors with robotics IP that was never commercially licensed
- University robotics labs with unlicensed patent portfolios (CMU, MIT, Georgia Tech, Stanford)

#### Segment 4: Connected Medical Devices / Digital Health

IoT-enabled medical devices generate patents in wireless protocols, data transmission, cloud analytics, and cybersecurity — all of which are practiced broadly outside healthcare.

**Target profile:**

- Remote patient monitoring companies
- Wearable device manufacturers
- Medical data platform companies
- Companies with patents covering Bluetooth Low Energy, LoRa, Zigbee, or cellular IoT in medical contexts

### Step 2: Research Each Target (Associate — 2-3 Hours Per Company)

For each Tier A and B target, the associate produces a **1-page Research Brief**:

**Section 1: Company Overview**

- Revenue, employees, products, market position
- Recent news (acquisitions, divestitures, layoffs, product launches, FDA actions)
- Any public statements about IP strategy

**Section 2: Patent Portfolio Summary**

- Total U.S. patents (Google Patents assignee search)
- Key patent families — identify 3-5 patents with the broadest claims
- Technology clusters (use CPC classification groupings)
- Expiration timeline — when do the most valuable patents expire?
- Forward citation analysis — which patents are most cited by others?

**Section 3: $1B Infringement Thesis**
This is the critical section. The associate must answer:

- Which specific claims, on which specific patents, read on which specific products?
- Who makes those products? (Name 5-10 companies)
- What is the aggregate annual revenue of those products?
- Does the total exceed $1B?

**Example thesis:**

```
[Company X] holds 12 patents in ultrasonic sensor fusion for surgical guidance.
Claims 1-4 of US Patent 10,XXX,XXX cover real-time 3D reconstruction from
ultrasonic array data — a method practiced by:

- Intuitive Surgical (da Vinci) — $7.1B revenue
- Medtronic (Hugo RAS) — estimated $200M+ in robotic-assisted surgery
- Stryker (Mako) — $2.1B in joint replacement robotics
- Siemens Healthineers (imaging-guided surgery) — $1.8B relevant segment
- Philips (image-guided therapy) — $1.2B relevant segment

Aggregate infringed revenue: ~$12B+. Thesis is strong.
Patent expiration: 2035. Sufficient remaining life.
No existing licensing program identified.
```

**Section 4: Decision-Maker Map**

- Who controls IP monetization decisions?
- VP/Head of IP → primary target
- General Counsel → secondary (often delegates IP commercial decisions)
- CTO → useful for technical credibility but rarely the deal decision-maker
- CFO → relevant when the pitch is framed as balance-sheet value
- CEO → for smaller companies ($100M-$500M), often the only decision-maker

**Section 5: Warm Path Analysis**

- Does Will know anyone at this company?
- Does Erich or anyone at SIM IP have a relationship?
- Does any Tier A attorney represent this company? (If yes, route through attorney referral, not direct outreach)
- Are any advisory board members connected?
- Has anyone from this company appeared at LES, AIPLA, or IPBC?

### Step 3: Will Reviews and Approves Targets

Associate presents the Tier A research briefs to Will in the Monday standup. Will approves, modifies the thesis, or kills the target. No outreach without approval.

## PART 3: OUTREACH SEQUENCE

This is a 10-week, 6-touch sequence. It’s slower and more relationship-oriented than the attorney outreach. You’re approaching a corporate executive about a strategic decision, not a law firm partner about a referral.

### Touch 1 (Week 1): The Insight Email

**Not a cold pitch. A demonstration that you’ve done the work.**

```
Subject: [Company Name]'s [technology] patents — untapped licensing potential

[Name],

I've been studying [Company Name]'s patent portfolio, specifically the
[technology area] family anchored by US Patent [X,XXX,XXX].

What caught our attention: the claims on [specific method or system]
read on products across [2-3 named sectors] — not just [company's
own market]. We estimate [N] companies generating over $[X]B in
aggregate revenue are practicing this technology without a license.

[Company Name] may not have the resources or appetite to run a
licensing program internally. That's what SIM IP does. We deploy our
own capital, handle all enforcement, and share revenue with the patent
owner. No cost to you. No disruption to your product business.

I'd welcome 20 minutes to walk through what we see. If the thesis
doesn't hold up, I'll tell you — and you'll still walk away with a
portfolio intelligence briefing you can use however you want.

Will Rosellini
[Title] | SIM IP
wrosellini@simip.io | +1 (305) 359-3650
simip.io
```

### Touch 2 (Week 2): LinkedIn Connection + Engagement

- Connect on LinkedIn with a personalized note referencing the email
- Engage with the target’s company or personal LinkedIn posts for 1-2 weeks before the next direct touch
- Do NOT pitch in the connection request. Just: “I sent you a note about [Company]’s patent portfolio. Happy to connect here as well.”

### Touch 3 (Week 4): The Data Drop

Send a brief follow-up with one new, specific data point the target didn’t have before.

```
Subject: Re: [Company Name]'s [technology] patents

[Name],

Following up with one data point that might be useful regardless
of whether we work together:

Your patent family [US X,XXX,XXX] has been forward-cited [N] times
— putting it in the top [X]% of patents in its technology class.
[N] of those citations come from [Company A], [Company B], and
[Company C], which suggests active R&D in your claim space.

This kind of citation density typically correlates with strong
licensing potential. Happy to share our full analysis.

Will
```

### Touch 4 (Week 6): Warm Introduction Attempt

Before Touch 4, check all warm paths. If a connection exists:

- Ask the mutual contact for an email introduction
- If the warm intro happens, skip Touches 5-6 and go directly to the meeting

If no warm path exists, send:

```
Subject: Complimentary portfolio analysis — [Company Name]

[Name],

I realize a cold email about patent monetization may not be top
of mind. So let me make this easy: our Garden Intel AI platform
can run a complimentary portfolio screen for [Company Name] in
72 hours. No cost, no commitment, no sales call required.

The output: a preliminary assessment of which patent families have
the highest licensing potential, who the likely licensees are, and
what the estimated revenue opportunity looks like.

If the numbers don't work, we'll tell you. If they do, we can
discuss next steps at your pace.

Want me to run it?

Will
```

### Touch 5 (Week 8): The Peer Signal

Reference a comparable transaction or anonymized case study.

```
Subject: How a [similar company type] turned dormant patents into $[X]M

[Name],

A company similar to [Company Name] — mid-market [sector], strong
patent portfolio, no licensing program — engaged SIM IP last year.

Garden Intel identified [N] high-value patent clusters covering
technologies practiced by [N] companies. Within [timeframe], the
portfolio was generating licensing revenue for the first time in
its history. The company's total investment: zero. SIM IP deployed
its own capital.

I believe [Company Name]'s portfolio has comparable or stronger
potential based on the claim analysis we've done. Happy to share
specifics.

Worth 20 minutes?

Will
```

### Touch 6 (Week 10): The Final Ask or Nurture Transition

```
Subject: Last note on [Company Name]'s patent portfolio

[Name],

I've reached out a few times about [Company Name]'s patent
portfolio. I'll keep this brief:

If the timing isn't right, I completely understand. I'll add you
to our monthly IP Monetization Briefing — a short market intelligence
email that's useful regardless of whether we work together.

If you'd like to revisit this conversation in the future, I'm easy
to find.

Best,
Will
```

**After Touch 6:** If no response, move to Nurture status. Add to the monthly value-add email list (Tier C equivalent). Revisit in 6 months or when a trigger event occurs (see Part 5).

## PART 4: POST-MEETING PROCESS

When a meeting is secured, the associate prepares a **Meeting Briefing Pack** for Will:

### Pre-Meeting (48 hours before)

1. **Full research brief** (from Part 2) updated with any new information
1. **3-5 specific patents** to discuss — with claim language and infringement mapping
1. **Decision-maker background** — career history, publications, conference appearances, LinkedIn activity
1. **Company context** — recent earnings, strategy shifts, M&A activity, leadership changes
1. **Objection prep:**
- “We don’t want to be seen as a patent troll” → “SIM IP acquires and monetizes. Your company’s name never appears in enforcement. We handle everything.”
- “Our patents aren’t that strong” → “Garden Intel’s preliminary analysis suggests otherwise. Let us show you the data.”
- “We tried licensing before and it didn’t work” → “What forum did you use? The UPC has changed the calculus dramatically. 14-month injunctions at 68% grant rate.”
- “We need board approval” → “Understood. We can provide a board-ready analysis with projected revenue ranges within 2 weeks.”
- “What’s the catch?” → “SIM IP deploys its own capital. You get upfront payment or revenue share. No downside cost.”

### Post-Meeting (Within 24 hours)

1. **Thank-you email** from Will — brief, references one specific point from the conversation
1. **Log meeting in Notion** — full notes, next steps, tier reassessment
1. **If evaluation was offered:** Submit Garden Intel request same day. Start 72-hour clock.
1. **If they need board materials:** Associate drafts a 2-page executive summary for the company’s internal use (SIM IP branded but written for their audience)
1. **If they declined:** Move to Nurture. Add to monthly email. Set 6-month revisit reminder.

## PART 5: TRIGGER EVENTS

These events signal that a company may be newly receptive to a portfolio monetization conversation. The associate monitors for these and flags them to Will immediately.

|Trigger Event                                 |Why It Matters                                                                           |Source                            |
|----------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
|**Layoffs or restructuring**                  |Companies look for non-operational revenue sources. IP monetization is an obvious lever. |SEC filings, news alerts, LinkedIn|
|**Product line divestiture**                  |The divested product’s patents may stay with the parent. Orphaned IP.                    |Press releases, M&A databases     |
|**CEO/GC/CTO change**                         |New leadership often audits IP assets. Fresh eyes = fresh appetite for monetization.     |LinkedIn alerts, press releases   |
|**Acquisition (company was acquired)**        |Acquirer may not need the full patent portfolio. Legacy patents become available.        |SEC filings, M&A databases        |
|**Patent expiration approaching**             |Companies with patents expiring in 5-7 years face a use-it-or-lose-it window.            |Google Patents, patent analytics  |
|**Competitor licensing program launch**       |If a competitor starts licensing, the target’s board will ask “why aren’t we doing this?”|IAM, ip fray, Law360              |
|**FDA clearance/approval of competing device**|A new entrant into the target’s market may be infringing the target’s patents.           |FDA 510(k) and PMA databases      |
|**IPO or SPAC**                               |Companies going public want to maximize all revenue streams.                             |SEC filings, EDGAR                |
|**Earnings miss**                             |Underperforming companies look for alternative revenue. IP monetization is non-dilutive. |Earnings reports, SEC filings     |
|**R&D budget cut**                            |Signals a shift from innovation to extraction. Monetize what you already have.           |10-K filings, analyst reports     |

### Monitoring Setup

- **Google Alerts:** Set up for each Tier A and B company name + “patent” + “IP” + “licensing” + “divestiture” + “layoff”
- **SEC EDGAR:** Quarterly check on 10-K/10-Q filings for Tier A targets. Look for IP-related disclosures in the risk factors section.
- **LinkedIn:** Follow all Tier A decision-makers. Job change = trigger event.
- **FDA databases:** Monthly scan of 510(k) clearances and PMA approvals in target technology areas.
- **ip fray, IAM, IP Watchdog:** Daily scan for competitor licensing activity.

## PART 6: NURTURE CADENCE (POST-SEQUENCE)

Companies that didn’t convert after the 10-week sequence enter the nurture pool. The cadence mirrors the attorney nurture workflow but with corporate-specific content.

### Monthly Value-Add Email (In-House Counsel Version)

Same format as the attorney email, but the lead insight is oriented toward corporate IP strategy rather than prosecution practice:

- “How [Company X] generated $[X]M from a patent portfolio they were about to abandon”
- “UPC enforcement data: what it means for medtech patent owners”
- “Three signs your patent portfolio is worth more than you think”
- “Why your competitor just hired a Chief Licensing Officer”

### Trigger-Based Re-Engagement

When a trigger event fires for a nurture-status company, the associate drafts a reactivation email:

```
Subject: [Trigger event] + your patent portfolio

[Name],

I saw [Company Name] [trigger event — e.g., "announced the
divestiture of its surgical navigation business"]. We spoke
[timeframe] ago about your patent portfolio's licensing potential.

This kind of transition often creates an opportunity to monetize
IP assets that may no longer be core to the business but still
have significant value to others in the market.

If the timing is better now, I'd welcome a fresh conversation.
Our Garden Intel analysis from [date] identified $[X]B+ in
infringed revenue across [N] companies — and that thesis is
likely stronger today given [market development].

Worth revisiting?

Will
```

### Qualifying Threshold

A target only enters the outreach sequence if the research brief demonstrates:

- Minimum 50 issued U.S. patents in a coherent technology cluster
- Identifiable claim coverage on products from 5+ third-party companies
- Aggregate infringed revenue estimate exceeding $1B
- Patent life remaining > 5 years on core families
- No existing licensing program or monetization partner identified

If a target fails any of these criteria, deprioritize to Tier C or discard. Don’t waste outreach touches on portfolios that don’t meet the acquisition thesis.
