## Named expertise

**Operational Systems Diagnostician.**

## Definition

The discipline of entering a complex, messy operating environment, reading it as a *system*
(incentives, workflows, constraints, root causes) rather than a list of complaints, earning the
operator's trust, and isolating the structural problem that ... if relieved ... moves the most.

The distinguishing trait is root-cause-over-symptom: he hears what an operator *asks for* and
diagnoses what they actually *need*, which are often different.

## Evidence (what he has actually demonstrated)

- **Absolute Mechanical discovery.** Sat with the owner of a ~30-person commercial HVAC contractor,
  mapped the real operating reality (TeamUp, Dropbox, QuickBooks Desktop, M365, paper, tribal
  memory), and correctly reframed the problem: the owner asked for "Gen AI on historical bids/
  margins"; Larry diagnosed that the real pain was structural workflow visibility. Right diagnosis,
  against the client's own framing.
- **PROMEON as externalized method.** Strip the development-economics costume and PROMEON's core is
  a codified diagnostic discipline: symptom vs. root cause, functional analysis, incentive analysis,
  isolate the binding constraint, don't jump to solutions. He didn't just diagnose once ... he wrote
  down a repeatable method for it. That is evidence of expertise, not a product to sell.

## What it is NOT (disambiguation ... prevents drift)

- **Not HVAC.** That domain belongs to the client (Chris Kellner). Larry is the diagnostician who
  walked in, not the trade expert.
- **Not software / building.** Every time he reaches for Supabase or wants to productize PROMEON,
  he is mistaking the costume for the craft. The build is the studio's job, not his.
- **Not "Market Systems Development."** MSD is the domain he learned the skill in. The skill itself
  ... systems diagnosis ... is domain-portable. Selling him as an "MSD expert" narrows him to
  development economics and misses the transferable value.

## Maturity / edge (where it's strong, where it stops)

Strong at *mapping*, weaker at *landing*. At Absolute he surfaced all the friction but proposed a
portal-wide scope ... a full system map, not the single binding constraint. He stops at "here is
everything that's broken" rather than "here is the one thing to fix first." That final cut ...
map → single highest-leverage intervention, then resist building the rest ... is the gap.

## Where it lands / market value (Credibility Map seed ... provisional)

Owner-led SMBs with messy, multi-system operations who feel the pain but have mislabeled it (they
ask for "AI" or "a portal"; they need a diagnosis). Larry's value to them is the trusted outside
read that names the real problem before anyone spends money building. In the studio model this maps
directly onto the SME seat: **carry the client, and guide what "good" looks like.** Per-segment
credibility detail to be formalized in a proper Credibility Map post-intake.

## Toward a product or service (explicitly TBD ... demand-first)

Do not lock this yet. The honest shape of a productized offering around this expertise is something
like a *diagnostic-as-a-service*: a fixed-scope operational diagnosis that names the binding
constraint and a smallest-viable intervention, with the studio building/running whatever it
prescribes. But that is a hypothesis. It only becomes real after a validated case proves a buyer
will pay for the diagnosis itself. Absolute is the first test of that.

## What the studio supplies (the partnership basis)

The complementarity is the whole reason the partnership could work: Larry maps and diagnoses; the
studio supplies the product-market discipline (isolate the one constraint, resist the full build)
and the system that delivers. His weak-at-landing edge is precisely the studio's strong suit. Two
halves of one offering.
