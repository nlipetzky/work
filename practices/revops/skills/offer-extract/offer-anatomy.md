# Offer Anatomy

The seven load-bearing sections of an offer. Used by `offer-extract` and any downstream skill that reads offer artifacts.

---

## Headline

One sentence. What is being pitched, to whom, why now. The headline is the test: if you cannot fit the offer in one sentence, you do not have an offer yet.

**Common pitfall:** writing a description of the play's vibe instead of the offer's substance. "We are going after the gene therapy market" is a play frame; "A GMP reagent qualification program for AAV process development teams scaling to commercial supply" is an offer.

---

## Audience

The persona/role being pitched, in human terms. Not a database segment.

**Common pitfall:** describing the audience by their attributes instead of their context. "VPs of operations at 200-person companies" is a database query; "Operations leaders responsible for analytical method development at AAV gene therapy companies pre-Phase 3" is an audience.

---

## The offer

What is being pitched, in substance.

**Common pitfall:** naming the delivery mechanism instead of the outcome. "A 30-minute consultation" is a mechanism; "A diagnostic of your reagent qualification timeline" is the outcome the consultation produces. Lead with outcome; mechanism is the secondary detail.

---

## Why now

Specific triggers that make this play timely. Time-bounded, observable, recent.

**Common pitfall:** evergreen why-nows. "The market is competitive" is true every year and therefore is not a why-now. "Three CGT manufacturers announced LA-area capacity expansions in the last 90 days" is a why-now.

---

## Proof

Verifiable evidence the offer delivers.

**Common pitfall:** unverifiable proof. "Trusted by leading companies" is not proof. "Currently supplying GMP reagents to [named company] for their commercial AAV manufacturing" is proof. If references must be anonymous, name the segment with comparable specificity ("a top-three CGT contract manufacturer producing for two FDA-approved therapies").

---

## The ask

The single next action we want the prospect to take.

**Common pitfall:** stacking asks. "Reply, book a call, or download the white paper" is three asks; the prospect picks zero. One ask, named clearly, sized for the audience at this point in the relationship.

---

## Out of scope

Explicit boundaries: what the offer is not, what it does not promise, who it is not for.

**Common pitfall:** treating boundaries as obvious. They are not. Without explicit out-of-scope, downstream copy drifts into adjacent territory and the play loses sharpness. The discipline of writing boundaries also surfaces ambiguities in the offer itself.
