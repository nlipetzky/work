# Outreach Offer-Construction Doctrine

Status: reference standard. No code.
Source of truth: NotebookLM "Cold Email" notebook (`a50bacaf-2622-4b27-b0a9-f2a24995449f`),
the Lead Gen Jay cold-email course (source `67895d46-6d7a-46d1-85fc-f802107d7d7b`).
Every claim below is distilled from that source. Where a framework is invoked but not taught
by the course, it is labeled EXTERNAL and excluded from the binding gates.

## What this is

The two existing doctrines (`cold-email-doctrine.md`, `linkedin-outreach-doctrine.md`) capture the
copy *form* (triple-tap, sentence count, no-link, sequence shape). They do NOT capture how to
*construct the offer the copy leads with*. This doc is that missing layer: the method for deciding
WHAT to offer cold, before a single line is written.

It is the certified standard the **offer producer** reasons from. Phase 2 of the outreach build
wires this doc into an `outreach-offer-ladder` artifact type as its `standard_rules` (deterministic
gate) and `standard_rubric` (LLM judge). The producer proposes a front-end offer + ladder; this
doctrine is what it is graded against.

Core thesis, in the course's own frame: the goal of cold outreach is NOT to sell the core offer.
It is to get the prospect to "raise their hand" — to opt in and say "yes, I'm a fit, tell me more."
The prospect is unaware, not searching, not hunting for you. You are interrupting. So the offer's
only job is to open the door.

## 1. The four offer-killers (hard-negative gate)

An offer dies cold if it is any of these. Each is a disqualifier, not a style note.

- **Boring** — "doesn't spark any excitement... they've seen it a million times." A generic,
  recognizable service framing. The prospect pattern-matches it to every other email and ignores it.
- **Competitive (commodity)** — "the same as everybody else in your space and there's nothing
  differentiating you." When the service is undifferentiated (SEO, paid ads, generic B2B lead gen),
  you compete only on price. Commodity = instant low reply.
- **Absurd** — "insane guarantees without any credibility." E.g. "100 leads in 30 days or your money
  back" / "six figures of revenue for you" with no social proof and no stated mechanism. Reads as a
  scam. "Instant spam box." If you make a claim, you must show the mechanism, ideally a unique one.
- **Complex** — "if I can't understand what it is, there's no way I'm going to take the time to try."
  If the offer can't be understood fast, the prospect marks it spam or archives it.

Corollary (copywriting don'ts the same source attaches to the offer): don't overpromise, don't be
vague about what you do or how, don't confuse the lead, never bait-and-switch. If the work is
genuinely complex, that is itself the reason to lead with a front-end offer and explain the rest on
a call.

## 2. The front-end-offer principle

Do NOT pitch the core / expensive / retainer offer cold. "People get defense mode immediately" when
pitched a generic service. The first touch's entire job is to "get your foot in the door" with a
hyper-specific front-end offer the prospect actually wants — usually a one-time thing, often at or
below cost. You are buying trust, not margin, on the first transaction.

How to find the front-end offer: take the broad/boring service, break it into bullet points of
everything it includes, and ask "which single one is the most desirable that nobody else is
focusing on?" Lead with that one bullet, not the umbrella.

## 3. The three front-end strategies

When the offer is boring/competitive/commodity (the most common case), use one of these:

- **Loss leader** — sell a specific, highly-desired micro-outcome at or below cost, priced to shock.
  Use when you know the exact high-value sub-deliverable the prospect wants. Example: instead of a
  monthly SEO retainer, sell a Forbes backlink the prospect assumes costs $6,000 for $1,000 ("how can
  you get that for $1,000? I'll definitely buy that"). The course's own example: a $97 done-for-you
  cold-email setup. Zero profit on the first sale; the back-end upsells/cross-sells fund it.
- **Trojan horse** — get in the door "under questionable pretenses," i.e. frame the touch as
  something other than a sales pitch (partnership, referral, interview). Use when pitching the actual
  service triggers immediate sales resistance. Example: a PR agency emails as a *journalist* who wants
  to write about the prospect's story, then transitions to sales once in conversation. Rule: not a
  bait-and-switch — there must be a smooth, honest transition to the real offer.
- **Reverse lead magnet (RLM)** — the modern replacement for dead "version 1" lead magnets (generic
  PDFs, videos, whitepapers, case studies — all tier-3, avoid). An RLM makes the prospect believe you
  are spending your own time and resources doing custom 1:1 work *for them specifically*, delivered at
  scale via AI automation. Use when you want to spike perceived value. The ask is the magnet: "I'd
  like to spend some time researching and putting this together for you — is that okay?" Perceived
  effort drives perceived value; the more time the prospect thinks you spent on them, the more
  valuable it reads.

Lead-magnet quality rule from the source: the more time the prospect *thinks* you spent on them, the
more valuable; the more time they must spend *consuming* it (reading a PDF, watching a video), the
less valuable. Big companies don't want to consume your PDF; they want the problem solved. Magnets
perform worse the further up-market you go — so up-market favors RLM-style custom work over downloads.

## 4. The offer ladder (ascension logic)

The front-end offer is never the business; it is the entry rung. The full ladder:

1. **Open the door** — front-end offer earns the reply and the trust. Zero profit expected here.
2. **Prove competence** — deliver the front-end offer and "knock it out of the park."
3. **Ascend to core** — having delivered undeniable value, the prospect is now open to an exploratory
   call where you "upsell what you really want to sell them": the core, high-ticket retainer. Back-end
   upsells and cross-sells pay for the whole system.

A valid ladder must therefore name: the front-end offer, the trust-proof delivery, and the explicit
path up to the named core offer. A front-end offer with no defined ascension to the core is incomplete.

## 5. Blue-ocean targeting (offer is inseparable from market)

The offer and the market selection are one decision.

- **Avoid red oceans** — "easy targets" abused by cold email: agency owners, doctors, lawyers, anyone
  assumed to have money and easy to find in Apollo. Inverse relationship: the more money a lead has,
  the *less* likely they reply (inbox saturation).
- **Target blue oceans** — ignored industries (manufacturing, farming), younger businesses (no
  entrenched vendors/loyalty), and newly-hired executives (e.g. a newly-hired CMO brought in to drive
  change — receptive by mandate). Balance: people with money who are *also* likely to reply.
- **Reposition a generic skill into a unique offer** — same skill, new framing for a specific blue
  ocean. "Rank you on Google" → "rank you on large language models" (train ChatGPT/Claude to name you
  #1 in your field). "Generic B2B lead gen" → "influencer outreach machines for ecom companies." Same
  capability, repositioned to something a specific underserved segment is receptive to.

## 6. Value equation — EXTERNAL companion (not from this course; not a binding gate)

The course explicitly invokes Alex Hormozi and aims for an offer that "would make even Alex from
Mozi proud," but the source does NOT actually teach his $100M Offers value equation. The variables
below are general public framework from Hormozi's external work, included only as an optional lens
when judging an offer's strength. They are NOT used as deterministic gates and must not be presented
as the course's method:

- Dream outcome (raise) · Perceived likelihood of achievement (raise) · Time delay (lower) ·
  Effort & sacrifice (lower). A stronger offer maximizes the numerator and minimizes the denominator.

Use this only as a soft tiebreaker. The binding standard is sections 1-5, which are sourced.

## 7. The gates this doctrine becomes (for the offer producer)

Translation of the above into the producer's two-tier check (Phase 2 wires these in verbatim-in-spirit):

Deterministic rules-gate (hard fail → revise):
- No absurd guarantee without a stated mechanism (no "X results or money back" without a named "how").
- No pricing committed that the expert has not approved (faithfulness; pricing is the expert's call).
- A front-end offer is named AND it is distinct from the core retainer (front-end ≠ core).
- The ladder names the explicit ascension path up to the named core offer.
- The offer is stated clearly and simply (a complexity/readability check).
- No red-ocean-only framing with no differentiation (must state what makes it unique / blue-ocean).

LLM-judge rubric (fuzzy quality after rules pass):
- Not boring, not commodity: does it spark interest and differentiate, or is it the thing they've seen
  a million times?
- Front-end strategy is correctly chosen and correctly applied (loss-leader / trojan-horse / RLM),
  with a clear, honest transition to the core (no bait-and-switch).
- Front-end offer is a single, hyper-specific, high-desire deliverable — not the umbrella service.
- Target market reads as a defensible blue ocean for this offer, not an abused red-ocean segment.
- The whole thing aims at a raised hand ("I'm a fit, tell me more"), not a cold close.
