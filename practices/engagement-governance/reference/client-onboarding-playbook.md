# Client Onboarding Playbook

Reference for any new engagement. Read before the first sales conversation. Apply religiously through kickoff. Re-validate quarterly.

The Teknova failure mode was every structural rule in the engagement-governance methodology either skipped or applied late, plus an unsurfaced engagement-type drift from outcome to implementation. This playbook prevents both.

---

## Step Zero: Identify the engagement type

Before the GTM Survey. Before pricing. Before any Trajectory drafting. Determine which kind of engagement this is.

### The three types

| | Outcome-based | Implementation-based | Hybrid |
|---|---|---|---|
| What client buys | A result (leads, meetings, pipeline) | A working system their team operates | Outcome now, capability transfer later |
| What operator owns | Full stack, indefinitely | Build, docs, training, handoff | Both, in phased contract |
| What client touches | Inputs and outputs only | The system, continuously | Phase-dependent |
| Success metric | Did the outcome materialize at agreed volume? | Can client operate independently? | Both, in sequence |
| Pricing logic | Retainer against outcomes | Project for build + retainer for support | Phased: outcome retainer then transfer fee |
| Expert role on client side | Minimal (operator IS the expert) | Heavy (client team being enabled) | Shifts at transfer point |

### Diagnostic questions for sales

Ask the prospect, in order:

1. **"When this is working a year from now, what does success look like for your team?"**
   - "We close more deals from leads landing in our CRM" → outcome
   - "Our team is operating the system you built" → implementation
   - "We start with you running it, eventually transition to us" → hybrid

2. **"What's your team's role once we're live?"**
   - "Approve copy, review lists periodically" → outcome
   - "Learn to operate the platform day-to-day" → implementation
   - "Phase one is hands-off, phase two is hands-on" → hybrid

3. **"If we produced the result via methods that worked but weren't AI, would that be acceptable?"**
   - "Yes, we just want the result" → outcome confirmed
   - "No, we specifically want AI capability" → implementation confirmed
   - "We want both" → push for which matters more

### Traps to watch for

- Outcome buyers will SAY "we want the AI capability" when they mean "we want the result and assume AI is how it happens." If you sell them implementation, both sides will be miserable.
- Implementation buyers will SAY "we want leads" when they mean "we want our team to generate leads using your tools." If you sell them outcome, they'll feel like you're hoarding capability.

### The decision

Document the type in the proposal. Build the Trajectory, pricing, artifact discipline, and cadence around that type. If the type shifts during delivery, treat it as a re-contract event, never a silent absorption.

## Step One: GTM Survey produces a Trajectory proposal

Will or whoever sells does not quote a retainer first. They quote a **Trajectory**. The Trajectory names:

- Engagement type (per Step Zero)
- Sponsor (named human, decision authority for scope)
- Expert (named human, owns artifact quality; can be same human as sponsor)
- Phases with explicit success and termination criteria per phase
- Tier (Solo / Multi / Full = 1 / 2 / 3+ sprints per week)
- Commercial structure: retainer amount, term, payment cadence
- Third-party cost model (client account, pass-through, or padded retainer)
- What's IN scope per month
- What's OUT of scope (mandatory list)
- Standing meeting cadence + standing weekly update channel
- Expert artifact deliverables required (written ICP, criteria, acceptance gates)
- Quarterly Trajectory review built in
- Scope-change protocol explicit in writing

### The hard rule

**No sponsor-approved Trajectory, no delivery work begins.** If a prospect resists Trajectory approval ("just start, we'll figure it out"), that's the diagnostic red flag. Decline or restructure. Clients who resist Trajectory approval will resist every structural rule later.

## Step Two: Kickoff (first week after signature)

### Joint kickoff session

- Sponsor and expert in the same room (if different humans) or with both interfaces named (if same human)
- Walk through Trajectory together
- Get explicit final sign-off in writing
- Confirm engagement type one more time, on record

### Operating setup

- Slot Report destination established (email, Slack, Notion, etc.)
- Standing weekly session time locked
- Scope-change protocol mechanics explained: 48-hour Scope-Change Notification with three options (add and re-price, defer, decline)
- Portfolio.md row created on operator side

### Expert enablement (if outcome) or learning plan (if implementation)

For outcome engagements:
- Hand the expert the relevant data-quality framework (e.g. outbound list quality reference)
- Set expectations on cohort-level feedback vs per-contact scrutiny
- Define what "good output" looks like in three KPIs max; sponsor signs off

For implementation engagements:
- Define what self-sufficiency looks like at end state
- Establish documentation cadence (artifacts ship with builds, not bolted on later)
- Define training plan: who learns what, when, by which milestone

For hybrid engagements:
- Both of the above, with explicit phase boundary defined

## Step Three: Hard rules to enforce from day one

These are discipline rules. They prevent Teknova from happening again. Enforce literally, not directionally.

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| No Trajectory → no work | Without an approved Trajectory, all scope is implicitly in-bounds and you're running unbounded |
| The first scope-change event sets the engagement pattern | First time a client asks for something off-Trajectory, the response defines the relationship. Always surface as Scope-Change Notification with three options |
| Sprint = unit of capacity, not "access to operator" | One sprint per week (Solo tier) is what they buy. Not your availability. Not your responsiveness. The sprint |
| Expert feedback in writing, always | Verbal feedback in meetings gets the response "send that to me in writing so I can scope it" |
| Engagement type re-validated at every quarterly review | Sponsor and operator explicitly confirm "is the engagement type still what we agreed to?" If drift, it's a re-contract event |
| Third-party costs handled per Trajectory model | Never quietly absorb variability. The model picked in Step One holds |
| Slot Reports name the bottleneck honestly | Including when the bottleneck is sponsor-side |

## Step Four: Ongoing discipline

### Weekly

- Monday: portfolio.md scan. Every active engagement has a locked sprint, an owner, and a recent Slot Report
- Tue-Wed: sprint execution
- Thu: standing session per engagement
- Fri: Slot Report delivered per engagement

### On-event

- Any scope-change request → Scope-Change Notification within 48 hours
- Any verbal ask → "send that in writing" before any work starts
- Any cadence slip → flag on portfolio surface within one week

### Quarterly

- Trajectory review per engagement with sponsor
- Engagement-type re-validation (per Step Zero)
- KPI review against original Trajectory commitments
- Re-approve, revise, or wind down

## Trajectory shapes by engagement type

### Outcome-based Trajectory

- Phase 1: Calibration (3-4 weeks testing to find the working motion)
- Phase 2: Production (steady-state outcome delivery at agreed volume)
- Phase 3: Optimization (refinement to push key KPIs)
- Phase 4: Renewal or wind-down

No "training your team." No "documentation deliverable." No "system handoff."

### Implementation-based Trajectory

- Phase 1: Discovery and architecture
- Phase 2: Build with phased sponsor reviews
- Phase 3: Pilot with client team operating under operator supervision
- Phase 4: Documentation, training, formal handoff
- Phase 5: Optional ongoing support retainer

Build deliverables ship with documentation attached. Documentation is not deferred to handoff.

### Hybrid Trajectory

- Phase 1: Calibration (outcome mode)
- Phase 2: Production (outcome mode)
- Phase 3: Capability transfer planning (sponsor decides to transition)
- Phase 4: Pilot with client team
- Phase 5: Documentation, training, formal handoff
- Phase 6: Optional ongoing support retainer

The Phase 2-to-Phase 3 transition is a re-contract event. Different pricing, different scope, different artifact discipline. Never silent.

## Slot Report shapes by engagement type

### Outcome Slot Report (weekly)

- This week's outcome metrics (meetings booked, pipeline generated, conversion rate, etc.)
- Trajectory phase and progress within it
- Active priority for next week (per sponsor)
- Bottleneck if any (ours, sponsor, expert, external)
- Open scope-change items

### Implementation Slot Report (weekly)

- This week's build deliverable shipped
- Documentation status against ship
- Client team capability progress
- Next sprint's priority
- Bottleneck if any
- Open scope-change items

### Hybrid Slot Report

Phase-dependent. Outcome shape during outcome phases. Implementation shape during build/transition phases. Phase header at top so sponsor always knows which mode applies.

## Drift detection (portfolio surface flags)

`portfolio.md` updated Mondays. Three flags catch drift early:

| Flag | Trigger | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Trajectory Stale | 3+ weeks of Slot Reports show drift from named phase | Schedule structured Trajectory review with sponsor within 2 weeks |
| Sponsor Disengaged | Sponsor hasn't responded to Slot Report or scope-change for 2+ weeks | Direct outreach naming the engagement risk |
| Sponsor-side Bottleneck | 2+ weeks of delivery held up by missing client inputs | Slot Report names it explicitly; escalation conversation if persists |
| Engagement Type Drift | Operator observes work shifting between outcome / implementation / hybrid without explicit re-contract | Halt unilateral absorption; surface as scope-change requiring sponsor decision |

## Mindset

Structural discipline isn't in tension with warm client relationships. It's what makes them sustainable.

A client who never wonders "is this in scope?" because the Trajectory makes it obvious, who never feels guilty asking for extra because the scope-change protocol gives them a clean way to choose, who can see exactly where the work is via Slot Reports, trusts the operator more, not less.

The friction Teknova produced was because of the lack of structure, not despite it.

Operator exhaustion in a Teknova-style failure is a willingness-to-overdeliver problem dressed up as a client-management problem. This playbook requires you to enforce the structures you've built. That's the discipline that turns the work sustainable.

## Quick reference checklist

Before contract signature:
- [ ] Engagement type identified (outcome / implementation / hybrid) and documented in proposal
- [ ] Sponsor and expert roles distinguished and named
- [ ] Trajectory drafted with phases, success criteria, termination criteria
- [ ] Tier selected; sprint count per week explicit
- [ ] Third-party cost model named in writing
- [ ] In-scope and out-of-scope lists drafted
- [ ] Scope-change protocol documented

At kickoff:
- [ ] Joint sponsor + expert session held; Trajectory approved in writing
- [ ] Cadence locked (Thursday session, Friday Slot Report)
- [ ] Expert handed relevant quality framework
- [ ] Portfolio.md row created

Ongoing:
- [ ] Monday portfolio scan completed
- [ ] Sprint locked per engagement
- [ ] Slot Reports sent Friday per engagement
- [ ] No verbal asks silently absorbed in past week
- [ ] No scope-change items past 48-hour decision deadline

Quarterly:
- [ ] Trajectory reviewed with sponsor
- [ ] Engagement type re-validated
- [ ] KPIs checked against original commitments
- [ ] Re-approval, revision, or wind-down explicit
